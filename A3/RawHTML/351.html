    Eight days into the Winter Olympics the U.S. is fifth in the medal count with nine over all—tied, along with Austria and Japan, with the Olympic Athletes from Russia, a team stripped of its flag and devoid of many of its stars due to doping sanctions.

    What You Need to Know Saturday

    U.S. DISAPPOINTMENT CONTINUES: Team USA suffered through another rough day Saturday, adding a single medal to a total that now stands at just nine. That’s less than half of Norway’s current haul of 21, and way behind Team USA’s showing at the same point in the 2014 Sochi Games. Germany has the most golds with nine. American short-track speedskater John-Henry Krueger salvaged the day for the U.S. late Saturday with a silver medal in the 1000 meters.

    VONN COMES UP SHORT: Lindsey Vonn was the latest star to falter. The veteran superstar, who is the most accomplished women’s Alpine skier ever, tied for sixth in the Super G after a wide turn on the lower half of the course cost her about a second. The 33-year-old Vonn’s quest to become the oldest female Alpine skier to ever win a medal now turns to the downhill, scheduled for Tuesday, where she may or may not race against teammate Mikaela Shiffrin. Stranger than Vonn losing was who won: the Czech Republic’s Ester Ledecka, who is more of a snowboarder than a skier and looked dumbstruck when she saw her time. Ledecka was so shocked to win that she wore goggles to her post-race press conference, because she wasn’t wearing makeup.

    FIGURE SKATING FEAT: Japan’s Yuzuru Hanyu became the first man in more than 60 years to win back-to-back gold medals in figure skating. Skating before an arena packed with shrieking Japanese fans, Hanyu topped a field that unleashed an onslaught of quadruple jumps, which were rare four years ago in Sochi but now must be performed multiple times by any serious competitor. But one of the top “quad” revolutionaries, 18-year-old American Nathan Chen, missed the podium. After a disastrous short program that left him in 17th place, Chen rebounded with a spectacular free skate in which he attempted a record six quadruple jumps, landing five of them cleanly. He posted the biggest score of the day, but it wasn’t enough to close the even bigger gap.

    WHAT TO WATCH (all times Eastern):

    • 8:15 p.m.: Alpine skiing continues with the men’s Super-G.

    • 11:15 p.m.: The men’s ski slopestyle finals, an event the U.S. swept in Sochi in 2014.

    • 6:05 a.m.: The 2-man bobsled begins with two heats on Sunday and concludes with two heats on Monday.

    • 6:56 a.m.: In speedskating, the women’s 500 meters is likely to come down to two rivals: Lee Sang-hwa of South Korea and Japan’s Nao Kodaira.

    A Surprise Silver for John-Henry Krueger

    Silver medallist John-Henry Krueger of the U.S. celebrates. Reuters

    GANGNEUNG, South Korea — American short-track speedskater John-Henry Krueger crossed the finish line, raised his hands to the sky and, when he skated around to his coaches, sank to his knees. Krueger had scrapped his way past into an improbable final of the 1,000 meters and survived a squeeze by two of the heavily-favored South Korean skaters. Now, with the Koreans behind him, he had his Olympic medal.

    Never mind that it was silver.

    Krueger, a 22-year-old from Pittsburgh, collected Team USA’s only medal of the day on Saturday in a race that saw three of the five finalists crash. He finished behind Samuel Girard of Canada, but had somehow beaten South Korea on their turf, four years after missing the Sochi Olympics due to swine flu.

    Seo Yira, who accidentally took out his own teammate, scrambled to his feet in time to skate across the line for bronze.

    The women’s 1,500 meters gave the 12,000 fans inside Gangneung Ice Arena more to shout about. Choi Min-jeong, the 18-year-old world record holder over the distance, claimed South Korea’s third gold medal of the Games, ahead of Li Jinyu of China and Kim Boutin of Canada. The biggest shock came when Shim Suk-hee, the highest-profile short-track skater in the country, crashed out less than halfway into her first heat of the night, sliding into the pads right in front of South Korean President Moon Jae-in.

    Japanese Star Hanyu Takes Gold, First Male Skater in 60 Years to Win in Back-to-Back Olympics

    GANGNEUNG, South Korea — Japan’s Yuzuru Hanyu defended his Olympic title, overcoming a mid-season injury to become the first man in more than sixty years to win back-to-back gold medals in figure skating.

    Hanyu's powerful performance included four quadruple jumps.

    Japanese fans who packed the arena for Hanyu — and Japanese reporters who had followed his presence since his security-guarded arrival here — were rewarded with a silver medal for Hanyu’s compatriot Shoma Uno, one of a handful of male figure skaters who have rocked their sport over the past four years with an explosion of quadruple jumps.

    The men’s final shattered a host of Olympic records, both in the technical content attempted by the skaters and the quality of their performances. Many doubled the number of quadruple jumps they had offered four years ago in Sochi, in performances that were also marred by significant errors even from the medalists.

    Hanyu’s training mate Javier Fernandez won a bronze medal, becoming the first Spaniard to win a figure skating medal. He did so despite coming from a country with a figure skating tradition so slender that it has been barely able to hold a national championships for much of Fernandez’s career.

    Nathan Chen, the American whose gold medal hopes were dashed after a disastrous short program left him in 17th position, fought back Saturday to set an international and Olympic record of his own by attempting six quadruple jumps in the free skate, and performing five of them cleanly.

    The performance rocketed Chen to fifth place overall, with compatriots Vincent Zhou and Adam Rippon all finishing within the top ten skaters.

    A Rebound for Nathan Chen

    Nathan Chen came back in the men’s free skate from the two worst short programs of his senior career to set a new international and Olympic record by attempting six quadruple jumps and landing five of them cleanly in his “Mao’s Last Dancer” long program.

    The 18-year-old Chen has been one of the drivers of the explosion of quadruple jumps in the men’s event in the past four years. For context, Yuzuru Hanyu won the 2014 Olympic title with one clean quadruple jump in his long program; he is expected to attempt four here later today, as may his chief rival, Shoma Uno, also of Japan.Chen set past high bars for the world by landing four, and then five quadruple jumps in his long programs.

    Chen’s program here earned him a score of 215.08 — potentially crushing for his rivals on a different day, but one that will likely still not be enough to earn him a medal after his 17th place finish in the short program. He currently has a total of 297.35.

    Still, he said, the performance was redemptive for him and came after he was buoyed by supportive messages that he could still go out and make the most of his time on Olympic ice.

    “I was just so low there was nothing I could lose at that point,” said Chen, who reeled off a quadruple lutz, two quadruple flips, two quadruple toe loops and a quadruple salchow, two of them in combination with other jumps, and with only one error on a quadruple flip, where he put a hand down on landing. He also secularly completed his triple axel, which had floored him in previous programs.

    Louise Radnofsky

    The Pride Flag Waves at the Olympics For Adam Rippon

    Getty Images

    GANGNEUNG, South Korea—Ian Rodriguez realized not long before the Pyeongchang Games that he would be in Asia with his husband during the Olympics. “Let’s go,” he said. Rodriguez bought tickets, changed his travel plans and went on Amazon to buy something he wanted to pack for his last-minute trip: a Pride flag with stars and rainbow stripes.

    Rodriguez, a 35-year-old X-ray technician from Las Vegas, brought that flag to the men’s figure-skating competition on Friday morning here and unfurled it after the short program of Adam Rippon, the first American figure skater to compete at the Olympics while openly gay.

    It was a remarkable sight: Rippon basking in the crowd’s support after the best skate of his life, even holding his hand to his ear to ask for more noise, as Rodriguez waved a flag representing equality in the Olympic arena.

    “He’s someone who represents my country and represents me,” Rodriguez said. “I feel like our community is not represented enough, and I’m proud that he has the balls to come out and be like: I’m gay, and I’m an Olympic athlete, and here I am. He doesn’t care what anyone thinks. He’s there for our community.”

    As recently as four years ago in Sochi, there were no publicly gay U.S. Olympians. There are now three—Rippon, freestyle skier Gus Kenworthy and speed-skater Brittany Bowe—and Rodriguez came to Pyeongchang in part to cheer for them specifically. “The fact that we have three athletes in the U.S. who are out and representing the LGBT community was even more of a push,” he said. “Everyone always comes out after they win their medal. And it’s like, well, it would be great if it was during the Olympics.”

    Rodriguez isn’t a diehard Olympics fan. “I don’t have everyone’s names memorized or anything like that,” he said. He barely knew who Rippon was before the Games began.

    It was through following Kenworthy that he learned of the flashy and sassy Rippon—the one who competes in a tight mesh shirt with shorter sleeves for the Olympics, once clarified that his butt was real, recently called himself a and that “I don’t want people to get distracted and forget how beautiful I am (on the outside)”—and Rodriguez knew he had to be there for what turned out to be a brilliant skate.

    Rippon, an unlikely first-time Olympian, found himself in seventh place as the top American skater after the short program on Friday, and Rodriguez celebrated by waving the flag he bought on Amazon. Figure-skating fans have long waved countries’ flags at international competitions and homemade banners for particular skaters at local events. But the Pride flag was so new that Rippon—one of three figure skaters competing here while openly gay, an Olympic first—couldn’t help but notice it when he qualified for Team USA at the national championships last month.

    “It’s overwhelming,” Rippon told The Wall Street Journal afterward. “It’s something that I’ve never seen before, and it’s something that’s so awesome.”

    Rodriguez brings his Pride flag wherever he goes in Pyeongchang. When he posted a photo of himself on Instagram displaying the flag at last week’s Opening Ceremony, he tagged people he thought would be interested, like Kenworthy, Rippon and Bowe. “I even put RuPaul,” he said.

    Kenworthy himself saw the notification on his Instagram and commented underneath Rodriguez’s post. “Thanks so much!” he wrote with three heart emojis.

    This is Rodriguez’s first time to the Olympics, like Rippon, and he didn’t know what to expect. He said he’s enjoying the Winter Games so much that he’s already planning a trip to the next Summer Games.

    Ben Cohen, Louise Radnofsky

    What You Need to Know on Friday

    U.S. MELTDOWN: The Olympics are all fun and games until your team’s two biggest stars stumble in their marquee event—at the exact same moment. That’s the nightmare that befell Team USA just before midnight Eastern time Thursday. Ski star Mikaela Shiffrin missed the podium in the slalom, while figure skater Nathan Chen stumbled badly in his short program, virtually assuring that he won’t win a medal.

    Shiffrin finished fourth in the slalom a day after winning gold in the giant slalom. Her finish was a huge surprise, given her dominance of the event in recent years. Competing on back-to-back days—with a medal ceremony in between that kept her up late—Shiffrin vomited before the first of two runs, fell behind and couldn’t make up the margin. She may skip her next scheduled event, the downhill, on Wednesday. The spotlight shifts to teammate Lindsey Vonn, the highly decorated Olympian who races in the Super-G in U.S. prime time Friday night.

    Just as Shiffrin finished, Chen crashed, literally. He fell on a quadruple lutz and then couldn’t land his quadruple toe loop. The 18-year-old, who was hoping to be the youngest men’s figure skating champion in more than 50 years, will instead almost certainly go home empty handed. The long program is on U.S. television this evening.

    THE SHIRTLESS TONGAN: In more uplifting news, shirtless Tongan flag-bearer Pita Taufatofua completed his obsessively chronicled transformation from Summer Olympics sideshow to Winter Olympics sideshow. Taufatofua, who competed in taekwondo at the 2016 Rio Games, finished the 15km cross-country race in 114th place—nearly 23 minutes behind the winner, but two spots ahead of his hotly tipped last-place finish. Taufatofua had never skied until last winter.

    Curling Order Gets Flipped Upside Down

    GANGNEUNG, South Korea—Things are topsy-turvy in the world of Olympic women’s curling.

    Canada and Switzerland, long the sport’s two dominant countries, are sitting at the bottom of the 10-country round robin. Meanwhile, sixth-ranked Japan has proven untouchable while host country South Korea, ranked eighth, is emerging as the giant slayer.

    On Friday, Team Canada lost a third straight match, this one to the ninth-ranked Danes, putting them on the verge of elimination in the round robin.

    Meanwhile, Korea has knocked off Canada and Switzerland, and only narrowly lost to its Japanese rival on Thursday night.

    To be sure, it’s still early in the ten-nation round robin. After each country has squared off against the other nine, the teams with the top four records will advance to the semifinals.

    For Team Canada, that means they’ll need to win the next six games to stand a chance of advancing to the medal round. For the record, Canada has never failed to win an medal in curling—men’s or women’s—since it was reintroduced to the Olympics at the Nagano Games in 1998.

    Canada has already taken gold in mixed doubles curling, which debuted last week in Pyeongchang.

    “Olympic Curling Shocker,” read the headline in Canada’s National Post after the women’s team’s second loss. “Nobody saw this coming,” it added.

    For South Korea to advance, the the underdog hosts need to keep their hot hand going, fueled in part by low expectations and robust support from a relatively rowdy hometown crowd.

    Through 2017, South Korea’s curlers were wrestling with internal turmoil within the national curling federation, including a leadership vacuum and an audit by the country’s sports ministry.

    Now, having taken down the top two teams, Korea arguably faces an easier road than Japan, which has so far faced only teams with lower rankings than its own.

    But as it stands, the nations set to qualify—Japan, Sweden, Great Britain and South Korea—rank No. 6, 5, 4 and 8 respectively, leaving the top three nations out in the cold.

    --Jonathan Cheng

    It's No Longer Just Fun at Games for Swiss Duo

    The Swiss Olympic team has gone from viral to virus.

    The squad best known for the wildly popular Instagram stunts by its freestyle skiers on Friday became the first at the Winter Olympics to confirm that the norovirus outbreak here had spread to its athletes, saying that two of them had been affected.

    Though the Swiss Olympic Committee didn’t reveal the identity of those athletes, one of them is widely believed to be freestyle skier Fabian Bosch, the 20-year-old who took the internet by storm last week with a video of himself dangling off an escalator one-handed. The clip was watched online more than half a million times.

    “The athletes concerned were immediately moved to single rooms and neither had contact with other athletes,” the team’s statement said in French. “They were also seen by the Swiss Olympic medical team and are no longer displaying symptoms of the virus.”

    Norovirus has turned into a daily talking point at these Olympics, ever since a spike in cases at a local youth center. As of Feb. 12, there had been 194 confirmed cases across the Olympic venues here, according to the Pyeongchang Local Organizing Committee. Some 47 people remained in quarantine.

    Until now, however, none of those people had been athletes. An outbreak in the Olympic village, where hundreds of competitors and team staff are living in close quarters and interacting at every meal, has the potential to be disastrous. But, as the Swiss pointed out, the affected athletes were not mixing much with the wider Olympic population since they were being housed in Bokwang, near the mountain freestyle venues.

    The team was also at pains to point out that the symptoms generally disappeared after 48 hours. “It runs its course quicker than the flu, for instance,” the statement added.

    While those two were now well enough to compete, according to Swiss officials, it’s fair to say that the most fun-loving team at these Games is now having slightly less fun.

    Joshua Robinson
