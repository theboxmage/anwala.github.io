What Is The Actual Monetary Value Of A PyeongChang Olympics Gold Medal?

The opening ceremony for the XXIII Winter Olympics Games in PyeongChang, South Korea, will begin tomorrow, marking the official start of the grandest event in sports. By its conclusion on February 25, nearly 3,000 athletes will have participated in a record 102 events in six sports and 15 disciplines. They will all be competing for the ultimate prize: The Olympic gold medal.

As a symbol of what is largely believed to be the ultimate accomplishment in sports, the gold medal is priceless. However, these medals do have an intrinsic monetary value.

PYEONGCHANG-GUN, SOUTH KOREA - FEBRUARY 14: Gold medalist Shaun White of the United States poses during the medal ceremony for the Snowboard Men's Halfpipe Final on day five of the PyeongChang 2018 Winter Olympics at Medal Plaza. (Photo by Quinn Rooney/Getty Images)

This year the PyeongChang 2018 Olympic gold medal has “podium value” of approximately $570 based on the spot price of gold and silver as of Thursday morning. This is a few dollars more than the gold medals at the last two Olympics in Rio and Sochi. However, it remains a long way off from $708 dollar record value of the gold medal at the 2012 London Olympics. While it is known throughout the world as the gold medal it is actually a silver medal plated with gold.

Getty Images

The reverse side of the Olympic Gold Medal detailing the discipline and event, alongside the official PyeongChang 2018 emblem

This year’s gold medal weighs 586 grams, making it the heaviest gold medal in Olympic history . Only 6 grams of the medal is made of gold (the minimum requirement under International Olympic Committee rules), while the remaining 580 grams is made of 99.9% silver (above the minimum requirement of 92.5% silver, which is sterling silver).

If the entire medal was made of gold it would be valued at approximately $25,000 based on current gold prices, which is why 1912 was the last time pure gold medals were awarded.

The silver medal weighs 580 grams, is made entirely of 99.9% silver (again above the minimum requirement) and has a podium value of approximately $313. The bronze medals weighs 493 grams, is made of 90% copper and 10% zinc, and has little intrinsic value.

Each medal is 92.5 millimeters wide, 109 mm long, and from 4.4 mm to 9.42 mm thick. A total of 259 sets of medals have been made.

The Medal Design

In addition to its monetary value, the Olympic medals serve as an artistic representation of both the Olympics Games and of the host country, which has the responsibility for the design and the manufacturing of the medals.

The obverse side has the Olympic rings while the reverse side details the discipline and event, along with the official PyeongChang 2018 emblem

The PyeongChang 2018 medals have a dynamic, modern design created by celebrated South Korean designer, Lee Suk-woo. He incorporated Hangeul—the Korean alphabet and the foundation of Korean culture—into the medal design through a series of consonants that stretch in three dimensional shapes across the face of the medal to its edge, where they come together to spell “Olympic Winter Games PyeongChang 2018.” Korean Olympic officials say these dynamic diagonal lines reflect both the history of the Olympic Games and the determination of the competitors vying for a place on the podium.

The design also resembles the texture of tree trunks, which organizers say symbolizes the work that has gone into both the development of the Korean culture and the PyeongChang 2018 Olympics.

Getty Images

The gold, silver and bronze medals

The obverse of the medals shows an offset depiction of the Olympic rings. The reverse features the name of the discipline and event, alongside the official PyeongChang 2018 emblem—a snowflake pattern created from a stylized representation of the hangul letters, ㅍ p and ㅊ ch, being the initial sounds of Pyeongchang (평창), according to Olympic officials. In addition, the former symbol represents the Korean philosophical triad of heaven, earth and humanity (천지인 cheonjiin), and the latter symbol represents a crystal of ice.

The ribbon is made of Gapsa, a traditional Korean fabric

This Hangeul emblem motif is continued through the light teal and pale red ribbon attached to the medal. The ribbon is made of Gapsa, a traditional Korean fabric.

The medals come with wooden disc-shaped containers that are curved to represent the shape of eaves that extend out from the roofs of Hanok, traditional Korean houses.
